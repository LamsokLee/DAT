Example 2

Mr. Alvarez is a 76-year-old man with diabetes who was struck by a car while crossing the road. He has many areas of bleeding located deep inside his brain, and is in a coma. He also has a broken leg and many broken ribs. One of his broken ribs has injured his lungs. While in the hospital, he gets an infection in his lungs (pneumonia). Mr. Alvarez:

  • Is an older man with some chronic medical problems, which are controlled
  • Has a complicated brain injury
  • Has other injuries to his body, including his lungs
  • Has an infection while in the hospital

The doctor thinks that Mr. Alvarez won’t be able to breathe or eat on his own any time soon, if ever. The doctor expects that his chance of surviving 6 months is low. He may improve to some degree, but will probably never be independent again. Whether he may go home ever again is unclear. It’s unlikely because he lives alone, and his children have their own families and jobs and won’t be able to look after him all day long. The doctor asks the daughter to help decide which goals of care best align with Mr. Alvarez’s values and preferences.

The daughter filled out the worksheet to help her decide which goal of care was best for her father:

Worksheet for Example 2, Mr. Alvarez


Part I: What is the decision and why do you have to make it?

1) What is the “goals of care” decision about?
“Choosing between survival at all cost versus comfort as the main treatment goal for my father. I need to think about what my dad would want.”
2) Why do you need to make a decision about your family member’s goals of care?
“Dad is in the ICU and his chance of surviving and being independent is low. He may die soon no matter what choice we make. He would definitely need to go to a nursing home if we choose survival as the goal. He would need a trach and a feeding tube..”

Part III: Your Goals of Care Options

Goals of care option

Reasons to choose this option

Reasons not to choose this option
“Do everything”: Focus on survival
Tracheostomy and feeding tube placement is done, followed by placement in LTACH or nursing home, sometimes acute rehab when the patient is ready.
  • Best chance to stay alive.
  • Patient will have a quality of life that he/she finds acceptable.
  • Patient has said in the past to keep him/her alive at all cost.
  • Patient would feel OK with being uncomfortable from lying in bed or working hard during therapy sessions.
  • Additional treatments can be painful.
  • Patient does not want to be dependent on others to live.
  • Patient does not want a tracheostomy or feeding tube.
  • Patient may still die and will have pain from additional treatments.
“No suffering”: Focus on comfort
Transition to comfort care is provided when the family is ready.
  • Unlikely that patient will have an acceptable quality of life.
  • Patient has suffered enough; family wants him/her to be comfortable.
  • Without life support, the patient will die.
  • Patient would choose to have advanced treatments and live in a nursing home.


3) How do you feel about this choice?
“My dad is a very independent person who loved life, walking to the library and playing chess with his friends. He likes to cook for himself and the whole family. He was already limited in how far he could travel due to his diabetes, and he did not like that. Now, his quality of life would be much lower. Without his chess or cooking, he would not want to just be alive. He would not want to go on with the outlook that he would be dependent on others. It makes me sad. I still want to talk this choice over with Dad’s doctor and my other siblings.”
