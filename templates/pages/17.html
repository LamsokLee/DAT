Example 1


Ms. Smith is a 35-year-old previously healthy woman who fell down the stairs. She is now unconscious. She was an avid runner. She was working full-time as an accountant. Because of her injury, she underwent surgery to remove blood from one area of her brain. She remains sleepy and confused during her ICU stay. She has a severe lung infection. Ms. Smith:

  • Is young and was healthy and active before her injury;
  • Has a severe brain injury;
  • Does not have any other injuries;
  • Develops a major infection while in the ICU.

The doctor thinks that Ms. Smith won’t be able to breathe or eat on her own any time soon. The doctor expects that it’s highly likely she will survive for six months or longer. She will probably improve to some degree, but it is not certain she will ever be independent or work again. The doctor asks Ms. Smith’s parents to decide which goals of care best align with Ms. Smith’s values and preferences.

The parents filled out the worksheet to help them decide which goal of care was best for their daughter:

Worksheet for Example 1, Ms. Smith


1) What do you understand about the outlook of your loved one, and the options?
“Overall the doctor thinks that she may improve, although there is a lot of uncertainty here. She will definitely need some form of LTACH or nursing home after the ICU, and then hopefully rehabilitation later. She will definitely need a trach and feeding tube before then. We understand the pros and cons of both options: survival or comfort care.”
2) What would your loved one want?
“Our daughter had a high school classmate before with a TBI and she knows what a trach/feeding tube look like. She was not opposed to it as long as she has a chance of getting rid of it. We know our daughter; she is a runner and has run marathons. This will be even longer than training for a marathon, but she will want to give it a try.”
3) How is this choice making you feel?
“We don’t like seeing her in pain, but at the same time from her marathon running she knows how to fight through the pain. We trust the doctors that they will do whatever they can to lessen the pain throughout the procedures.”
4) What questions do you need answered before you can decide?
“We have a few questions regarding the procedures themselves and how to get her to the next level of care after the ICU. We have written down the questions and are meeting with the doctor to get them answered. We know that the Social Worker and Case manager will talk to us about what the next steps are regarding finding a place for after the ICU and acute care hospital.”
5) Imagine a line where the right side is comfort care and the left side is doing everything possible for survival. Where do you think your loved one would put themselves on the line below?
“We are leaning towards continuing advanced medical care and going forward with the trach and feeding tube and what follows after the ICU stay. We know it’s going to be a long, painful road ahead, but we know our daughter’s endurance and will power. She would accept a lower quality of life and keep fighting to make the best of her life.”
