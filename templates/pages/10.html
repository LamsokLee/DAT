WHEN SURVIVAL IS THE GOAL OF CARE


  • Doctors and nurses will do whatever it takes to keep a person alive.
  • Patients will require additional procedures to improve their chances of survival.

Patients usually need these two procedures:

{% if session['accordion1'] == True %} {% else %} {% endif %} 1. Tracheostomy (“trach”)

For the first days after a major TBI, patients are not able to breathe on their own. They depend on a breathing tube placed through the mouth and hooked up to the ventilator. If patients are still unable to breathe on their own after several days, a procedure called a tracheostomy is done to avoid serious long-term damage to the windpipe.

During a tracheostomy, a surgeon makes a hole in the front of the patient’s neck, allowing direct access to the windpipe. A shorter tube is placed through the hole, and the ventilator is hooked up to it. Over time most patients can slowly discontinue the ventilator, but they will keep the trach for several weeks to months. Once the ventilator is removed, the patient breathes oxygen or regular air through the trach.

Patients can’t speak with a trach until their condition improves. When a patient is well enough, a cap can be placed over the opening. Many but not all patients can have their trach removed after weeks or months. A small scar remains where the hole was.

{% if session['accordion2'] == True %} {% else %} {% endif %} 2. Feeding Tube (“PEG” or “G-tube”)

A patient can’t eat by mouth when on a ventilator or with a trach. Therefore, a surgeon places a feeding tube through the skin directly into the stomach. Liquid nutrition and medications are administered through the feeding tube.

Usually, the trach and feeding tube surgeries are done at the same time. The feeding tube may stay in longer than the trach. In some cases, people may have to use a feeding tube permanently, if they don’t recover enough to eat on their own.

Both the trach and the feeding tube are removable if the patient’s condition improves enough.

If your family member does not recover to the level you had hoped, despite the highest level of care, you may decide to have the trach and feeding tube removed at that point and transition to comfort care.

Some patients die despite the best efforts of the doctors and the medical team and despite additional procedures to keep them alive.
