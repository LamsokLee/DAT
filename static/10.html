WHAT IS TREATMENT THAT FOCUSES ON MAXIMIZING SURVIVAL?

When the goal of care is survival, doctors do whatever it takes to keep a person alive. Patients will require additional procedures to improve the chance that they survive. Patients usually need these two procedures:

1. Tracheostomy (“trach”): For the first several days after a major TBI, patients are unable to breathe on their own. They depend on a breathing tube placed through the mouth and hooked up to the ventilator. When patients are still unable to breathe on their own after several days, a procedure called a tracheostomy (short: “trach”) is done to avoid serious long-term damage to the windpipe that can be caused by a breathing tube when kept too long.

For a trach, a surgeon makes a hole in the front of the patient’s neck to directly access the windpipe.

A shorter tube is then placed through this hole, and the ventilator is hooked up to it to help the patient breathe. Over time most patients can slowly come off the ventilator but they will keep the trach for several weeks to months. Then they breathe oxygen or regular air through the trach.

Patients can’t speak with a trach until they are well enough to place a cap over the opening. Many but not all patients can have their trach removed after weeks or months. A small scar remains where the hole was.

This patient has a tracheostomy (“trach”). On the left side is the trach without an oxygen mask (“trach mask”); on the right side is the same patient with a trach mask through which he breathes oxygen or air.

2. Feeding Tube: (also called a “PEG” or “G-tube”). A patient can’t eat by mouth when on a ventilator or with a trach. Therefore, a surgeon places a feeding tube through the skin directly into the stomach. Liquid nutrition and medications are given through the feeding tube. Usually, the trach and feeding tube placement are done together. Even if patients are eventually able to be without a trach, the feeding tube may stay in for a longer time, sometimes for the rest of the person’s life, depending on whether they recover enough to eat on their own.
