Example 2

Mr. Alvarez is a 76-year-old man with diabetes who was struck by a car while crossing the road. He has many areas of bleeding located deep inside his brain, and is in a coma. He also has a broken leg and many broken ribs. One of his broken ribs has injured his lungs. While in the hospital, he gets an infection in his lungs (pneumonia). Mr. Alvarez:

  • Is an older man with some chronic medical problems, which are controlled
  • Has a complicated brain injury
  • Has other injuries to his body, including his lungs
  • Has an infection while in the hospital

The doctor thinks that Mr. Alvarez won’t be able to breathe or eat on his own any time soon, if ever. The doctor expects that his chance of surviving 6 months is low. He may improve to some degree, but will probably never be independent again. Whether he may go home ever again is unclear. It’s unlikely because he lives alone, and his children have their own families and jobs and won’t be able to look after him all day long. The doctor asks the daughter to help decide which goals of care best align with Mr. Alvarez’s values and preferences.

This is an example of how the daughter may fill out the worksheet to help her decide about which general goal of care is best for her father:

Worksheet for Example 2, Mr. Alvarez

1) What do you understand about the outlook of your loved one, and the options?
“The chance of surviving and also being independent again is low. He has a high chance of dying soon no matter what choice we make. He would definitely need to go to a nursing home if we choose survival as the primary goal. He would definitely need a trach and feeding tube. There are definite pros and cons for both decisions. It makes me sad. But I need to think about what my dad would want.”
2) What would your loved one want?
“My dad is a very independent person who loved life. He loved walking to the library and playing chess with his friends. He liked to cook for himself and the whole family during family gatherings. He was already very limited in how far he could travel and go due to his diabetes, and he did not like that. Now, his quality of life would be much much lower. Without his chess or cooking he would not want to just be alive. He would not want to go on with the outlook to be dependent on others.”
3) How is this choice making you feel?
“It makes me very sad, but at the same time, I know I have to decide what’s right for my dad, and I need to decide what he would want.”
4) What questions do you need answered before you can decide?
“I have to talk to my brother and sister about this to find out what they think. I don’t feel pressured about this. I don’t want anyone to think I gave up on Dad though. I don’t think I’m giving up but I worry about this some. But I believe that Dad’s beliefs and mine are pretty similar. I know we have to consider his wishes and quality of life.”
5) Imagine a line where the right side is comfort care and the left side is doing everything possible for survival. Where do you think your loved one would put themselves on the line below?
“I’m leaning toward avoiding prolonged life support and invasive procedures like trach and feeding tube. He would never want to be in a nursing home, not even temporarily. I still want to talk this choice over with Dad’s doctor and my other siblings.”
